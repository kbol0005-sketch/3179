Australia’s Domestic Air Travel Recovery After COVID-19

Australia’s domestic air network connects key hubs such as Sydney, Melbourne, and Brisbane with regional destinations across the country. The visualisation below integrates an interactive map and route-level passenger trends to illustrate how traffic volumes have evolved between 2019 and 2024. We focus on comparing these two years as representative pre- and post-pandemic benchmarks, since the early post-pandemic period (2021–2023) remained distorted by gradual reopenings, capacity constraints, and uneven demand recovery.

While most domestic routes experienced passenger changes within a normal range, some major corridors showed pronounced swings of up to ±30%. These substantial increases and decreases highlight how travel behaviour shifted in the wake of the pandemic patterns that readers can explore further using the interactive filters and traffic-change categories below.

Available Seat Kilometres (ASKs), Revenue Passenger Kilometres (RPKs), and Load Factors (LF) show how much capacity airlines supply and how effectively it’s used. The sharp dip in 2020 reflects the pandemic’s impact on domestic travel, followed by a strong rebound from 2021 onward.

The chart above visualises annual passenger traffic at Australia’s major airports between 2015 and 2024. It highlights the sharp decline during the pandemic years, followed by a steady recovery across most airports. Sydney, Melbourne, and Brisbane have now returned to near pre-pandemic levels, while smaller airports such as Hobart and Canberra show slower but consistent growth. This trend reflects the broader rebound of domestic tourism and business travel across the country.

This dashboard was created using Vega-Lite and Pure.css as part of an academic visualisation project. Data source: BITRE (2024).
